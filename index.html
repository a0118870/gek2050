POPMUSIC & WOMEN
Do women in popular music lyrics tend to be presented in a stereotyped way? This digital project aims to address that question by coding the lyrics of the top 30 songs of 2015 as listed in the Billboard Hot 100 within the paradigm of objectification versus empowerment.

DISCUSSION

Lyrics are obtained from an online source that also provides user-contributed and verified artiste-contributed comments on the meanings exuded by the lyrics. Coding is done based on the information provided from the source and also through my own inference of the lyrics. The coding categories are: (-2) Sex Object, (-1) Promiscuity, (+0) Lyrically Neutral, (+0) Lyrically Ambiguous, (+0) Not Gender Specific, (+1) Important Person, (+1) True Love, (+1) Mutual Love, (+1) In Control, (+2) Independence, (+2) Female Dominance and (+3) Seen as Equals. Songs are scored based on the points of the coding categories. A negative score would signify objectification whereas a positive score signifies empowerment.

It is to be noted that the coding of the lyrics are not an absolute objective view on the nature of women stereotyping it represents. This is due to potential misunderstanding of cultural differences in language and the use of pseudonyms and hidden meanings that may or may not be intended by the artiste. For example, #4 Trap Queen tend to refer to vice-related activities in its lyrics but the image portrayed is one of women empowerment through independence and as equals.

However, the analysis still shows an interesting pattern particularly towards songs sung only by females. The lyrics of these songs lean towards empowerment rather than the objectification of women. When mixed with male artistes however, the lyrics tend towards objectification. This phenomenon might be a result of the nature of the male artiste's lyrics and would require further in-depth analysis through artiste profiling. Lyrics by male artiste are not clearly defined towards either ends of the research paradigm and tend to remain neutral.

Due to time constraints, the small purposive sample may have resulted in an unfair analysis as there is no balanced mixed of gender for this study (19 males, 7 females, 4 mixed gender). Furthermore, a temporal scope of 1 year disables the potential for studying trends in lyric stereotyping especially in recent years where gender equality is a frequently discussed topic. Categorizing the songs by the artiste's gender may not also provide a proper analysis as compared to categorizing them by the songwriter's gender as the artiste may not have a say in the actual song writing process.

A further study could include a much wider scope as mentioned earlier and could also focus on genres on top of gender. A comparison of popular music lyrics in different languages and/or countries may also prove fruitful in understanding culture tolerance and acceptance of certain values and stereotypes as the narrow scope on the United States of America would not provide a universal understanding of stereotypes in popular music lyrics as compared to those found in countries that are more reserved such as China.
